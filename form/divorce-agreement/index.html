Divorce Settlement Agreement Template

Use our divorce settlement agreement to settle divorce details outside of court.

Updated September 29, 2023 | Legally reviewed by Brooke Davis

A divorce settlement agreement (or a marital settlement agreement) helps two divorcing spouses by detailing the division of assets and spousal support. If the former couple has children under 18, this agreement may also record details about expected child support and custody.

…

A divorce agreement typically addresses:

  • The division of assets and debts.
  • Alimony arrangements.
  • Child custody provisions.
  • Child support terms.

What Is a Divorce Settlement Agreement?

A divorce settlement agreement is a written document between separating spouses that outlines, with specificity, all their agreements concerning the division of their property, assets, and debts. It also describes their arrangements for the custody, care, and support of their children, if any, once their divorce becomes final.

In the absence of a prenuptial agreement, the partner with the higher income is typically obligated to provide financial support to the other, encompassing both alimony and child support.

Using a divorce settlement checklist can help you understand what you need to plan for before you start writing your agreement.

How to Write a Divorce Settlement Agreement

Here’s a list of steps on how to write a divorce settlement agreement:

Step 1 – Provide Parties and Marriage Information

The first section of a divorce settlement agreement should contain the details of both parties and their marriage.

Petitioner and Respondent Information

The petitioner is the spouse who filed the divorce petition. The respondent is the spouse who has been served the divorce petition. You should include the contact details of both the petitioner and the respondent. Each party should specify whether they’re a United States Armed Forces member.

If you’re a United States Army, Marine Corps, Navy, Air Force, or Coast Guard member, you will be covered under the Uniformed Services Former Spouses’ Act (USFSPA). The USFSPA discusses how to treat retirement pay, commissary, exchange, and health benefits. It also advises on issues such as child support and spousal support.

A court would consider whether either spouse is a member of the armed forces when deciding if the settlement is fair.

Former Name

If one party wants to restore their former or maiden name, they can make that request in this agreement.

Marriage Date and Location

You should specify when you and your spouse got married and the location.

Current Status

For no-fault divorces, some states require that the parties have been living “separate and apart” for a certain amount of time before a divorce can be granted. Establishing the date of separation in this agreement can help confirm this period if required.

Remember that living “separate and apart” does not necessarily mean the parties must live in different places. At its minimum, it means that they cannot be physically intimate. One spouse is often monetarily disadvantaged in the relationship, so living separately is not always possible until the divorce is granted.

Step 2 – Provide Spousal Support Information

Spousal support, or alimony, is payment from one spouse to another after a separation or divorce. Spousal support allows the receiving spouse to maintain the standard of living they had during the marriage.

Specify which party will be receiving spousal support, the amount, the date payments will begin and end, and whether it can be modified.

If one spouse must pay spousal support, it is common practice for the supporting spouse to carry a life insurance policy to guarantee payments if they die. The receiving spouse would be named the beneficiary, and the supportive spouse generally must maintain the insurance policy as long as spousal payments are required.

Step 3 – Add Children’s Information

Mention if the parties have any children under 18 or whether they’re expecting any children. You should also include the custody arrangement between the parents:

  • Sole legal and physical custody
  • Shared legal custody and primary physical custody
  • Shared legal and physical custody
  • Sole legal and shared physical custody

Legal custody determines which parent has the right to make decisions regarding the child(ren)’s upbringing, such as medical care, education, and religious affiliation. If a parent has sole legal custody, the parent can make unilateral decisions regarding the child(ren) without consulting the other parent.

Physical custody determines which parent(s) the child(ren) will live with and the agreed-upon schedule. The parent with physical custody provides day-to-day care.

Step 4 – Agree on Visitation Arrangements

Visitation gives the non-custodial parent the right to visit their child(ren) regularly. The non-custodial parent will be entitled to regular visitation if the parents have shared physical custody. Even if one parent has sole physical custody, the non-custodial parent will still be granted regular visitation unless extenuating circumstances exist.

The visitation schedule can include overnight weekday visits, certain weekend hours, and/or certain holidays and celebrations.

Step 5 – Decide on Children’s Expenses

A couple with an impending divorce can decide on children’s expenses, such as:

Children’s Medical Insurance

It would be best to discuss with your spouse which of you will be responsible for your child’s medical insurance and how any medical expenses not covered by the insurance will be paid.

Child Support

Child support is monthly payments made by one spouse to the other spouse to cover the financial needs of any children they may have. Both parents should support such children, and courts can obligate one party to pay child support.

Each state has child support guidelines that help guide how much a spouse should pay. Based on these guidelines, child support amounts help the court determine whether the amount is fair. Although the parties do not need to use the guidelines, if a court is asked to approve the agreement, it may ask for the basis of how the amount was determined.

If a parent needs to make changes to a court-ordered child support agreement, they can use a Child Support Modification form.

Children’s Tuition Expenses

You can include provisions for tuition expenses for your child. You can specify who will pay for their private school or college tuition and expenses.

Special Rights and Responsibilities

Special rights and responsibilities are agreements between the parents regarding the care of the child(ren) and any actions to be taken (or not to be taken) while the child(ren) is in the care of the other party.

For example, you can specify that neither parent can encourage their child to refer to another person as “Mother” or “Father” when they separate.

Step 6 – Add Property Information

Your marital settlement agreement should include information about the following property:

Real Property

This section is about the marital home. The marital home is the home the couple lived in together when they were married. When the parties separate, they must decide what to do with the marital home. The parties can agree that:

  • One party will keep the marital home and be the sole owner.
  • They will sell the home and split the profits.
  • They will continue to own the home jointly, and one party will continue to reside there.

Personal Property

Smaller items such as furniture, accessories, pots, pans, and dining ware are usually divided in advance among the parties and referred to in the agreement. These are generally less valuable items.

However, larger and more valuable items of personal property can be divided in the agreement. Things such as a painting, crystal chandelier, vintage dining table, or other items of value that may be a source of contention between the parties or have significance for one spouse can be agreed upon and divided in the agreement.

Vehicles

If the parties jointly own vehicles, they should decide how they will be split in the divorce and how the vehicle note, if applicable, will be paid.

Step 7 – Provide Debts, Accounts, and Tax Information

Former spouses can decide on how to account for the following finance-related accounts and debts:

Bank and Financial Accounts

If you and your spouse share a joint banking account, you should fill in the account details here. A joint banking account is a checking, savings, money market, certificate of deposit, or other account held at a financial institution in the name of both parties. The parties must decide how they want to divide any joint banking accounts.

Outstanding Debts, Charge Accounts, and Credits

Make an agreement with your spouse on who will be responsible for any outstanding joint debts and provide details of the debt in the document.

Pensions and Retirement Accounts

Spouses may also be entitled to a portion of the other spouse’s pension and/or retirement plans, which are accounts created or opened in the present to replace income upon retirement. If money was paid into the account while the spouses were married, it might be considered part of the marital estate.

Examples of such plans include Individual Retirement Plans (IRAs), 401(k)s, and profit-sharing plans.

Decide if you or your spouse will divide any interests into the pension and retirement accounts and provide the details of the retirement accounts of the relevant parties.

Income Tax

Regarding income tax, you and your spouse must decide when both of you will start filing individual tax returns. It can be at the beginning of a particular year or when the divorce decree becomes final. Additionally, discuss who will be responsible for any deficiencies from prior joint tax returns.

Step 8 – Add in the Final Details

As you finish writing your marital settlement agreement, you can ensure to account for the following details:

Waiver of Payment through Clerk

In some states, spousal support by default is paid through a Central Depositary Payment Program or a court clerk, direct deposit program, or other third party. Such a system allows the court to ensure that payments are being made.

The parties can waive the right to have payments made through a Central Depositary Payment Program and, instead, agree to make payments directly to each other.

Additional Provisions

This section allows you to add further terms to the agreement not mentioned in the prior sections.

Witnesses & Notary Acknowledgements

You don’t need to have any witnesses sign the agreement. However, having witnesses sign will help prove the document’s authenticity, should it ever come into question. Be sure to check your state’s laws regarding any witness requirements.

You should get the agreement notarized by a notary public.

Filing

Divorce agreements are filed with the court, and the court must approve the document before granting a final divorce decree. The agreement is usually merged into the final divorce decree, where it becomes enforceable by the court.

If you do not merge it into your final divorce decree, the agreement is enforceable in the same ways as any other legal agreement between you and the other party. A divorce settlement agreement filed with the court is generally called a marital settlement agreement.

Lastly, add the agreement date to finish composing your divorce settlement agreement.

Tips for Writing a Divorce Settlement Agreement

Here are some tips to implement when writing a divorce settlement agreement:

Consider Using a Mediator

As you write your divorce settlement agreement, consider whether mediation would be a suitable option for you. Discussing issues with an impartial third party can help resolve them quicker, and you can avoid having the divorce process drawn out in court.

Also, depending on the age of your children, mediation could help your kids have a say in their custody arrangements. Speaking frankly with either parent may be too difficult for them.

Using a mediator may result in more honest responses and ultimately lead to you creating an agreement that satisfies the entire family.

Fully Disclose All Information

When writing your divorce settlement agreement, listing all assets (including gifts, inheritances, and real estate property) is essential. Failure to disclose all of your assets could result in future litigation where you could receive fines or have to potentially transfer ownership of the asset to your spouse.

Consider that some states follow community property laws while others are strictly separate property states. Before writing your joint divorce papers, consult a qualified legal professional to ensure you follow state divorce laws.

Review Your Agreement Carefully

Review your agreement carefully to ensure you didn’t omit any sections or provide incomplete information. If you forget to address a topic in your divorce settlement agreement, it may be more challenging to handle once you and your former spouse are already separated.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens if I violate a divorce settlement agreement?

If a spouse does not follow the provisions mentioned in the divorce settlement agreement, the other spouse may have the right to file a motion for contempt. The judge will then assess the circumstances and decide whether the spouse violated the decree’s orders. In this case, the judge might impose penalties such as a hefty fine or jail time.

Can I create my own divorce papers?

Yes, you can create your divorce papers. You can create your divorce papers when the property division is fair and reasonable and with acceptable custody, parenting times, and support arrangements.

The papers are usually just formalizing the initial separation or divorce settlement agreement. However, having an attorney review the documents before the court would be best.

Can a divorce settlement agreement be overturned?

Yes, you can overturn a marital settlement agreement in certain instances. You can request a post-judgment modification when your circumstances have changed and the terms of your agreement no longer reflect your situation. For example, one partner might have suddenly lost their job and can’t keep up with child support payments.

The court will usually not amend the agreement if the change concerns property division.

Alternatively, you can file an appeal if you think the judge has overlooked evidence or if you disagree with their findings. You can only file the appeal within 30 days of the judge signing the document, and you will be required to prove that the judge made a mistake.

What paperwork do I need to file to ask for a divorce?

‌ ‌You should check with your state and country to determine the required documents, as they vary by state. For example, some states need you to provide Affidavits of Financial Means or Confidential Information Sheets. But the most common documents are:

  • Dissolution of marriage petition
  • Settlement agreement
  • Parenting plan (if you have children)
  • Court cover sheet if needed
  • Summons to be served on your spouse (petitioner serves the respondent who has a certain number of days to respond, or a default judgment may be given)
