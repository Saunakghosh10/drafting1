Horse Bill of Sale Form

Use our Horse Bill of Sale to record the sale of a horse and protect both buyer and seller.

Updated July 2, 2023 | Legally reviewed by Brooke Davis

A Horse Bill of Sale, or equine bill of sale, documents the sale or transfer of a horse between a seller and a buyer. When buying a horse or pony, a bill of sale for a horse shows that ownership of the equine has been officially transferred to a new person.

Once signed, this document is a legally binding agreement that protects the buyer and seller if either party changes their mind or disputes arise.

What is a Horse Bill of Sale?

A horse bill of sale is a legal document that memorializes the sale of a horse between two parties – a seller and a buyer. The seller agrees to transfer horse ownership to the buyer in exchange for a certain amount.

A horse bill of sale is an official record that a horse was sold on a certain date for a certain price.

Horse Bill of Sale Requirements

A simple Horse Bill of Sale is proof that the purchase occurred and includes the following details:

  • Seller: the person who currently owns the horse being sold
  • Buyer: the person buying the horse or becoming the new owner
  • Description of Horse: the horse’s date of birth, coat color (bay/chestnut/gray), sex, breed, registration number, markings or tattoos
  • Sire: details about the horse’s father, including his registration number
  • Dam: details about the horse’s mother, including her registration number
  • Price: state the sale price and how the horse will be paid, whether in full or in installments, plus when the registration papers will transfer hands
  • Warranties: whether the horse is sold “as is” or if there are guarantees
  • Date: when the horse was sold by the Seller to the Purchaser

How to Sell a Horse

Selling a horse can be a complex process, but here are some general steps to get started:

Step 1 – Determine Your Horse’s Value

Research the market to find out how much horses of similar breeds, ages, and training are selling for. Consider your horse’s physical condition, temperament, training level, and competition history.

Step 2 – Advertise Your Horse

Use online classifieds, social media, horse forums, and local newspapers for advertising your horse. Be sure to include high-quality photos, videos, and a detailed description of your horse.

Step 3 – Prepare Your Horse

Ensure your horse is well-groomed and healthy before showing them to potential buyers. Consider having your horse professionally trained or taking them to competitions to increase its value.

Step 4 – Screen Potential Buyers

Once you have potential buyers, ask them about their experience, riding goals, and why they are interested in your horse. Consider conducting a background check or requesting references to ensure the buyer is legitimate and reliable.

Step 5 – Show Your Horse to Potential Buyers

Schedule a time for the buyer to meet your horse in person. Allow the buyer to spend time with your horse, ride them, and ask any questions they may have.

Step 6 – Negotiate the Sale

Once the buyer has expressed interest, negotiate the price and terms of the sale.

Step 7 – Prepare a Horse Bill of Sale

Once the buyer and seller have agreed upon the sale terms, create a horse bill of sale. The bill of sale should include the horse’s name, breed, age, color, markings, registration number, and unique characteristics.

It should also include the date of sale, the purchase price, the method of payment, and any conditions of the sale, such as warranties or guarantees.

Step 8 – Sign the Horse Bill of Sale

The buyer and seller should sign the bill of sale to acknowledge the transfer of ownership. Ensuring that a third party, such as a notary public, witnesses the signatures is essential.

Step 9 – Exchange Payment and Documents

Once the bill of sale is signed, exchange payment for the horse. Provide the buyer with all necessary documents, such as the bill of sale, registration papers, and health records.

Step 10 – Keep Copies of the Bill of Sale

The seller should keep a copy of the bill of sale for their records, and the buyer should receive a copy as proof of ownership.

How to Write a Horse Bill of Sale

When writing a horse bill of sale, simply follow the below steps:

Identify the buyer and seller: Include the buyer and seller’s full legal names, addresses, and contact information.

Horse information: Provide a detailed description of the horse being sold, including the horse’s name, breed, age, height, color, gender, and any distinctive markings.

You should also cover the breeding details such as the registration number, date of birth, name of the sire and the dam, and their registration numbers.

State the purchase price: Clearly state the purchase price for the horse. You may also include any agreed-upon payment terms, such as a deposit or installment payments. Include whether the sales tax is included in the price and the payment method.

Outline any warranties/guarantees: If the seller is providing any warranties or guarantees related to the horse, such as the horse’s health or suitability for a specific purpose, this should be included in the bill of sale.

Include any conditions of the sale: If there are any conditions that must be met before the sale can be completed, such as a veterinary exam or transportation arrangements, these should be included in the bill of sale.

Sign and date: The buyer and seller should sign and date the bill of sale, and each party should keep a copy for their records.

Consider including a notary statement: Including a notary statement can provide additional legal protection and help verify the document’s authenticity.

Why Do You Need a Bill of Sale For a Horse?

There are several reasons why you need a bill of sale when buying or selling a horse:

Proof of ownership: A bill of sale proves that the seller legally owns the horse and has the right to sell it. This protects the buyer from purchasing a stolen or illegally obtained horse.

Transfer of ownership: A bill of sale indicates that ownership of the horse has been transferred from the seller to the buyer. This protects the seller from future claims of ownership or disputes about who owns the horse.

Legal protection: A bill of sale outlines the terms of the sale, including the purchase price, payment method, and any warranties or guarantees.

This provides legal protection for both the seller and the buyer, ensuring that both parties understand their rights and obligations under the sale agreement.

Registration and insurance: A bill of sale may be required to register the horse with breed associations or obtain insurance. Without a bill of sale, the buyer may be unable to prove the horse’s ownership.

Required by state: In some states like California, Florida, and Kentucky, a bill of sale is legally required whenever a horse is sold, purchased, or transferred.

Frequently Asked Questions

What should a horse bill of sale include?

A simple horse bill of sale should generally answer the following basics:

  • Who is buying and selling the horse
  • What the purchase price of the horse is for future tax purposes
  • Where the horse was registered and its registration number
  • When the horse is being purchased
  • How the horse is sold with warranties and conditions or simply “as is”

A horse bill of sale may also address these additional considerations:

  • Breeding Rights: whether rights to breed the horse are being transferred
  • Commission: whether the buyer and/or seller will pay an agent, trainer, or third party a fixed amount or percentage of the purchase price for help with brokering the deal
  • Deposit: whether and under what conditions the seller must refund the deposit if the buyer rejects the horse after confirming the horse’s health with a veterinarian
  • Registration Papers: confirm when the official registration papers should be transferred to the buyer and make sure the horse description and registered owner match
  • Veterinarian’s Examination: whether the buyer can get a pre-purchase veterinary exam with a blood sample from a local vet, especially if the horse is sold “as is”

Is a horse bill of sale necessary for all horse sales?

A horse bill of sale is not legally required for all horse sales, but it is strongly recommended for both the buyer and the seller. It provides legal protection and clarity in any disputes or issues.

How should the purchase price be determined when selling a horse?

The purchase price for a horse can be determined by various factors, such as the horse’s age, breed, training, and health.

It is essential to consider the market value of similar horses and any additional costs or expenses associated with the horse, such as shipping or veterinary care.

Should a veterinarian be involved in the sale of a horse?

It is recommended to have a veterinarian involved in selling a horse, mainly for pre-purchase exams.

This can help ensure the horse is healthy and free from any underlying medical conditions affecting its value or performance.

Are there any state-specific laws or regulations that should be considered when selling a horse?

Yes, there may be state-specific laws or regulations that should be considered when selling a horse. For example, some states may require a specific type of bill of sale or may have regulations related to horse sales or transportation.
